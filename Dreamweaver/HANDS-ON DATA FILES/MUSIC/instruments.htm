Instruments

Different types of sounds are made from different instruments. The vibration produced from playing an instrument produces sound waves in the air. The sound waves travel to our ears and cause our eardrums to vibrate in the same pattern as the instrument being played.
Some instruments include trumpet, trombone, horn, tuba, clarinet, saxophone, bagpipes, accordion, harmonica, flute, drums, bass, banjo, piano, violin, guitar, harp, cello, cymbals, triangles, xylophones, gongs, chimes, and rattles. Cultures all over the world have created musical instruments by using the natural materials of their region (fiber, metal, reed, gut, skin, and stone) to produce sound.

Anthropologists have classified all musical instruments in five basic categories:

Aerophones are instruments that produce sound by a vibrating column of air. These usually consist of a pipe made out of wood, metal, or plastic, sometimes with a reed attached. Examples are flute, oboe, and clarinet.

Idiophones are wood or metal percussion instruments that produce sound by being struck, scraped, or shaken. Examples are gongs, cymbals, rattles, and xylophones.

Membranophones are instruments that produce sound by striking or rubbing a skin or membrane stretched across a resonating air chamber. Examples are conga drums and timpani.

Chordophones are instruments that produce their sound through the vibration of strings by being vowed, plucked, or struck. Examples are guitar, harp, violin, and string bass.

Electrophones are instruments that produce sounds from electricity. Examples are electronic organs and synthesizers.
